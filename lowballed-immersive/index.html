Black and Latino families for decades have seen their homes appraised for tens of thousands - or hundreds of thousands - of dollars less than expected. Many suspect racial bias is a factor.

An ABC Owned Television Stations analysis of data for more than 50 million home loans shows refinance applications in predominantly Black neighborhoods across the country are nearly five times more likely to be under-appraised than homes in white communities.

The exclusive ABC analysis also found a similar pattern when people are buying and selling homes: loans in Black neighborhoods are more than twice as likely to be appraised under value.

The investigation found the profound impacts. More home loans fall through. The gap in home ownership between white families and people of color persists. And lost generational wealth.

A 2018 report from the Brookings Institution estimates an astounding price tag. The researchers say that homes in predominantly Black neighborhoods are devalued by $156 billion, an average of almost $50,000 per home.

That's enough money to pay for 8 million four-year degrees and cover nearly all of the damage from Hurricane Katrina.

This is vital equity Black families lack access to in order to pay for college, start businesses, remodel homes or create a nest egg for future generationsâ€”creating intergenerational wealth.

The ABC analysis found that while everyone's homes are subject to a possible under-appraisal during the home-lending process, it's happening more often to Black and Latino people nationwide - in neighborhoods in almost every community.

As a neighborhood's share of Black or Latino residents rises, the share of lowballed appraisals rises. The gap is also higher in the poorest neighborhoods. The data analysis shows that appraisers were as much as 30 times more likely to undervalue homes in America's poorest Black neighborhoods than in the wealthiest white neighborhoods.

The result is a growing number of Black and Latino families are taking steps to prevent, or prove, the suspected bias. They're "white-washing" their homes by removing personal belongings - from family photos to hair products to anything that could be a potential signal that the home they're refinancing or selling belongs to a family of color. They say they're doing it to increase their chance of landing a fair appraisal.

Experts in the home-lending and appraisal industry who've studied the issue for years say it's a systemic nationwide problem that's backed up by the data the government gathers about almost every home loan application.
